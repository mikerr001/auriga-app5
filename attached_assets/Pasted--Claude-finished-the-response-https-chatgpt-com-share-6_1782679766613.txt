Bearing Engine — Converts a detected object's horizontal pixel offset from center + camera's known FOV into a left/right angle, then buckets it into LEFT/SLIGHT_LEFT/CENTER/SLIGHT_RIGHT/RIGHT for speech output.

Hazard Engine — Takes object class + distance + bearing + velocity (if trackable across frames) → computes severity, probability, confidence, and a composite risk score → plus a separate prediction sub-engine that computes time-to-collision for moving hazards (vehicles/people on a collision course), distinct from static obstacles.

Guidance Engine — Turns hazard+bearing+distance output into actual spoken instructions ("turn until beep," movement toward a checkpoint, periodic distance updates, organic hazard warnings) — this is the layer between raw detection and human-usable speech.

Audio/Haptic Engine — Silent-by-default speech system (only speaks when needed or asked), volume/priority that escalates with danger, one-earbud mode preserving environmental awareness, directional haptic vibration codes as a backup channel especially in DND/loud situations. Virtual Fiducials, explained as used in this code
A Virtual Fiducial is not a physical marker you print and stick on a wall (the way ArUco/QR-style fiducials work in robotics). It's a computed reference scale derived purely from a detected object's known or estimated real-world size — "virtual" because no marker physically exists; the object Auriga already detects becomes the measuring stick.

The math is the classic pinhole camera model, implemented in VirtualFiducialEngine.estimateDistance():

distance_meters = (real_world_width_mm × focal_length_px) / detected_pixel_width / 1000
Intuition: the further away an object is, the smaller it appears in the frame. If you know roughly how wide something is in real life (a person ≈ 450mm shoulder-width, a car ≈ 1800mm), and you know your camera's focal length, then the only unknown left is distance — and the ratio of real size to apparent (pixel) size tells you exactly that.

In the actual pipeline (AurigaPipeline.kt):

MediaPipe detects an object and gives a bounding box (in pixels)
TYPICAL_WIDTHS_MM looks up an assumed real-world width for that object class
CameraCalibrationManager supplies the real (or fallback) focal length for this specific phone
VirtualFiducialEngine combines these into a distance estimate, plus a confidence score that gets penalized for things like low light, partial occlusion, or the object being unusually small/large in frame (suggesting an unreliable detection)

Trust & Credibility Mechanics
The core problem stated precisely: a sighted person evaluating a productivity app can screenshot the UI, read reviews, and form a judgment in minutes. A blind person evaluating Auriga's hazard-detection claims cannot independently verify accuracy the way a sighted reviewer could — they can't watch a demo video and assess whether the bounding boxes are correctly placed, can't read a screenshot-heavy blog review and judge for themselves. They're entirely dependent on either trusted intermediaries or lived experience over time. This is a structurally different trust problem than most consumer software faces, and it means your usual playbook (app store screenshots, feature-comparison charts, influencer unboxings) does almost none of the actual trust-building work it would for a sighted-user product.

What actually builds trust in this category, in rough order of weight:

Other blind users, especially ones with standing in the community. This is by far the heaviest-weighted signal. Blind and low-vision communities are tight, interconnected, and highly word-of-mouth-driven specifically because most other trust mechanisms (visual review, marketing claims) don't work for them — they've had to build their own peer-trust infrastructure out of necessity. A recommendation from a respected blind community member is worth more here than almost anywhere else in consumer tech.
Disability advocates, O&M (orientation and mobility) instructors, and accessibility professionals. These are the people blind users already go to for "should I trust this tool" — not influencers in the generic sense, but professionals whose job is literally evaluating assistive tech credibility. Getting even one O&M instructor or vision rehab professional to genuinely try Auriga and speak positively about it (not paid endorsement — genuine use) is worth more than a large general-audience marketing push.
Accessibility-focused tech journalists/reviewers within the blind community (the kind of person who wrote that detailed, critical Lookout review we found earlier — accessibleandroid.com is exactly this kind of outlet). These reviewers are trusted because they're known to be critical, not promotional — they'll point out real flaws, which is precisely what makes their positive coverage meaningful when it happens. Pitching to outlets like this only works if your product can actually survive scrutiny — which loops back to why soft-launching quietly before any press push matters so much.
Schools for the blind and disability NGOs, which you've already identified as a channel — these double as trust infrastructure, not just distribution. A school recommending Auriga to its students carries institutional weight individual reviews don't.
Generic tech press / general consumer reviews — genuinely low-value here, possibly even risky. A mainstream tech outlet reviewing Auriga without disability-community context might focus on irrelevant things (UI aesthetics, generic "AI app" framing) and miss what actually matters, or worse, could inadvertently overstate capability in a way that erodes trust with the community that matters once they see the gap between press claims and lived reality.
The specific mechanism I'd build deliberately: an early "trusted tester" cohort, not a generic beta.

Rather than a broad open beta, identify and personally reach out to a small number (5-15) of people who sit at the intersection of "blind or low-vision" and "has some standing/voice in the community" — could be a blind YouTuber/podcaster, an O&M instructor who is also blind, an active member of a blind community forum or WhatsApp group. Give them real, hands-on early access, ask for honest (not necessarily positive) feedback, and fix what they find before going wider. Their honest experience, communicated through their own existing trust networks, is your marketing — far more than anything you could produce yourselves.

One uncomfortable thing worth saying directly: because you can't currently verify your own hazard-detection accuracy with real-world testing (per KNOWN_RISKS.md — no real device testing has happened yet), you are not actually in a position to make trust-building claims to this audience yet. The sequence has to be: build → test rigorously with sighted internal testing first → then a small trusted-tester cohort of actual blind users in controlled, safe conditions (not "trust us, walk around with this") → only then wider release. Skipping ahead to community outreach before the product is genuinely reliable risks burning trust you can't easily rebuild in a community this interconnected — a bad early experience shared in the right WhatsApp group could do lasting damage before you've even properly launched.

Metrics & Success Criteria
The core trap to avoid first, stated plainly: standard app metrics (DAU, session length, retention curves) can actively mislead you in this specific product category, and you need to decide now how you'll interpret them, or you'll make bad decisions later when the data looks "bad" but actually isn't.

The metric-conflict problem, concretely
If Auriga works well, a user who was anxious and over-reliant in week 1 should, by week 8, walk familiar routes with more confidence and need fewer spoken interventions — meaning less app engagement is potentially a sign of product success, not failure. This is the exact opposite of how retention/engagement is normally read in consumer software, where declining usage = churn risk = bad. If you measure Auriga the way you'd measure a social app, you'll see "engagement decay" and panic, when what actually happened is the user got more independent — which is the entire point of the product.

This means you need two separate metric tracks that you deliberately don't conflate:

Track A — Business health metrics (the normal SaaS stuff, still necessary)

Install → activation rate (did they get through permissions + first successful hazard detection)
D1/D7/D30 retention
Free → paid conversion (once monetization is live)
Churn, broken down by reason if you can get it (this matters more than the raw number — churn because "I don't need it as often anymore" is fine; churn because "it gave a wrong warning and scared me" is a crisis)
Track B — Safety/outcome metrics (the ones that actually matter to your mission, and that no generic analytics dashboard gives you for free)

Hazard detection accuracy in the field (true positives, false positives, false negatives) — ideally validated against some ground truth, even informally early on (trusted testers reporting "it warned me about X and X was/wasn't actually there")
Time-to-warning before contact, for hazards that did occur
Self-reported confidence/independence change over time (a simple periodic in-app prompt: "compared to a month ago, do you feel more, less, or about as confident navigating new places?") — this is a survey metric, not a behavioral one, and it's the one that can validate "less usage = more confidence" rather than just guessing at that interpretation
Critical-alert-to-near-miss-avoided ratio, if you can ever get qualitative reports of this (hard to instrument cleanly, but even anecdotal trusted-tester reports are worth collecting deliberately, not just informally)
What "Phase 1 successful enough to move to Phase 2" should actually look like
Given the above, I'd define Phase 1 (free launch) success as a combination gate, not a single number:

Reliability gate: false-positive/false-negative rate from trusted testers is low enough that people aren't losing trust in warnings (you need to define a threshold here once you have real data — this is currently unknown and unmeasurable until real device testing happens)
Retention gate, interpreted correctly: D30 retention that's stable or only gradually declining (gradual decline consistent with growing independence is fine; a sharp early drop-off is not — sharp early drop-off after 1-2 uses signals something broke trust fast, not growing confidence)
Qualitative gate: at least a handful of genuine, unsolicited positive testimonials or community mentions — this is your actual green light for Phase 2 messaging, since Phase 2 marketing depends on having real stories to tell, not just internal metrics looking fine
Don't move to Phase 2 on Track A numbers alone looking decent if Track B is unknown or bad — a free product with growing installs but unreliable hazard warnings is a liability, not a success, and rushing to monetize on top of that compounds the risk (you'd be asking people to pay for something not yet proven safe).

One important practical point: instrument this from day one, not later
Your ContinuousLearningLite correction-logging system (predicted vs. corrected class) is part of this infrastructure, but it's not sufficient alone — it only captures object-classification corrections, not "did the hazard warning actually matter, was it timely, did the user trust it." You'll likely want a very lightweight, occasional in-app prompt (not constant, that would be annoying and counter to "silent by default") asking something like "was that warning helpful?" after a subset of hazard events — a simple thumbs up/down, voice-answerable. Legal & Regulatory
Data residency and privacy law — the part that actually bites first
You're not yet handling much sensitive data in the current architecture (Auriga's core pipeline is offline-first, local Room storage, camera frames processed and discarded rather than uploaded) — but two things change that picture meaningfully, and both are already in your roadmap:

The cloud LLM tier sends conversational voice-query text off-device to a third-party API. The moment that's live, you're a data controller/processor under whatever law applies, full stop.
The family check-in feature we discussed involves a backend relay between two users' devices — meaning you'd be storing and transmitting some data (even if deliberately minimal — last-active timestamps, SOS events) about a vulnerable user population to a third party (the linked family member).
Kenya specifically has a real, operative data protection law — the Kenya Data Protection Act (2019), enforced by the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner — modeled significantly on GDPR principles (consent, data minimization, purpose limitation, breach notification). This isn't a hypothetical future concern; if you're collecting any personal data from Kenyan users (which voice queries and family-check-in data would constitute), you're likely already in scope once those features go live, regardless of how small you are. Practically, for a small startup, the realistic exposure isn't "Kenyan regulators will descend on a small free app" — it's more about (a) doing right by your own stated values, (b) not creating legal liability that surfaces later if you grow or seek institutional partners who'll ask about compliance, and (c) building good habits before you expand to more jurisdictions with sharper enforcement.

If/when you expand to Nigeria, the Nigeria Data Protection Act (2023) is the equivalent — broadly similar GDPR-derived structure, separate regulator (NDPC), separate registration/compliance obligations. India has the Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023) — again broadly GDPR-influenced but with its own specific consent and grievance-redressal mechanics, and India is a meaningfully larger, more aggressively enforced regulatory environment than Kenya at this stage, so if South Asia expansion ever becomes real, budget real compliance attention there specifically, not just a policy copy-paste.

The practical near-term move, given you're not building yet but should plan for it: when the cloud LLM and family-check-in features get built, the architecture should default to data minimization as a design constraint, not an afterthought — don't log voice query content anywhere persistent beyond what's needed to generate the response, don't store more family-check-in detail than the specific reassurance signals we scoped, and write a real (not boilerplate) privacy policy that accurately describes what little you do collect. A short, honest, specific privacy policy is also, not coincidentally, a trust-building asset in exactly the way we discussed under credibility mechanics — "we don't store your conversations, here's exactly what we do keep and why" is a stronger trust signal than a vague wall of legal boilerplate.

Medical device classification — the question worth taking seriously, not dismissing
This is the one most founders in assistive tech underestimate, so let's be precise about it rather than hand-wavy.

The general regulatory principle (US FDA, EU MDR, and most other frameworks broadly agree on this structure even where specifics differ): software becomes a regulated medical device when it's intended to diagnose, treat, mitigate, or prevent a disease or medical condition, or when it processes patient-specific data to inform a clinical decision. Pure assistive/accessibility technology that helps a person with an existing, already-diagnosed condition navigate daily life generally falls outside medical device regulation — this is the same reason a white cane, a screen reader, or Seeing AI/Lookout/Be My Eyes aren't regulated as medical devices. You're not diagnosing blindness or treating it; you're helping someone who already knows their visual status navigate the world.

Where the line gets genuinely closer to risky, specifically for Auriga's roadmap: if you ever add features that could be read as making a clinical claim — e.g., anything framed as detecting deterioration in someone's vision, providing data intended to inform a doctor's treatment decision, or (more relevantly given your "Continuous Learning Lite" and correction-logging infrastructure) any feature that aggregates and presents data as if it were diagnostic insight ("your detection-correction patterns suggest your vision may be changing") — that kind of claim could plausibly tip into device-adjacent regulatory territory in some jurisdictions. Right now, nothing in your roadmap does this, and I'd recommend treating "we do not make any clinical or diagnostic claims, ever" as a hard product-copy rule going forward, not because you're currently at risk, but because it's an easy line to accidentally cross with well-intentioned future feature ideas (e.g., "track how your environment-detection accuracy changes over time" sounds harmless but starts adjacning toward health-monitoring framing if worded carelessly).

Bottom line for now: you're not a medical device, and your current and near-term roadmap doesn't push you toward becoming one. The actionable takeaway is a marketing/copy discipline, not an engineering one — never claim Auriga diagnoses, monitors, or informs treatment of any vision condition, only that it helps people who are already blind/low-vision navigate more safely.

What "Virtual Fiducial" actually means, conceptually
In classical machine vision, a fiducial marker is a physical object of known size placed in a scene specifically so a camera system can use it as a reference — think of the black-and-white ArUco/QR-style square markers used in robotics and AR. You print it, you know it's exactly 100mm wide, and because you know its real size and you can measure its size in pixels, you can back-calculate distance using simple geometry.

The "virtual" in Virtual Fiducial is the key conceptual leap: instead of placing a known marker in the world, you treat objects Auriga already detects — people, doors, cars, chairs — as if they were fiducial markers, using typical known real-world dimensions as a stand-in for "the marker's actual printed size." Nothing physical is added to the scene. The object being detected anyway becomes the measuring reference, after the fact, computationally.

This is genuinely clever for your use case specifically because: a blind user can't be expected to place physical markers in their environment before walking around. The entire system has to bootstrap distance estimation from what's already there.

The math, walked through properly
This is the pinhole camera model — one of the oldest and most fundamental relationships in computer vision, and it's genuinely simple once you see why it works.

The physical intuition first, no equations yet: hold your thumb at arm's length — it covers maybe a building in the distance. Move that same building twice as far away, and your thumb would cover two buildings' worth of width. Objects shrink in your visual field proportionally to their distance. This relationship is exact, not approximate, for a simple camera/lens model — and that exactness is what makes the math work.

The formal relationship:

distance_meters = (real_world_width_mm × focal_length_px) / detected_pixel_width / 1000
Breaking down each term:

real_world_width_mm — how wide the object actually is in real life (a person's shoulders ≈ 450mm, a standard door ≈ 800mm). This is the "virtual fiducial" input — the assumed known size standing in for a physical marker's known size.
focal_length_px — a property of the camera, not the scene. It describes how strongly the lens "compresses" the 3D world onto the 2D sensor. This is why CameraCalibrationManager matters — get this number wrong for a specific phone's camera, and every distance estimate is proportionally wrong, consistently, for that device.
detected_pixel_width — how wide the object appears in the captured image, in pixels — this comes straight from the object detector's bounding box.
Why the relationship is "inverse": the formula is real-width × focal-length, divided by pixel-width. As an object gets farther away, its pixel-width shrinks, and since pixel-width is in the denominator, a shrinking denominator means the resulting distance grows. That's exactly the relationship that matches physical reality — smaller apparent size means farther away — and the formula just makes that relationship precise and computable rather than just intuitive.

Where the actual engineering risk lives, and why your architecture already addresses some of it
The formula itself is exact given accurate inputs. The real-world accuracy problem is entirely about how good your three inputs are, and this is worth being precise about because it's where Virtual Fiducials succeed or fail in practice:

real_world_width_mm is the weakest input, by far. Real people range roughly 350-600mm shoulder-width; assuming a flat 450mm for every "person" detection means your distance estimate for an unusually narrow or wide person is proportionally wrong in exactly that ratio. This is the limitation we already documented honestly in the README and KNOWN_RISKS.md — TYPICAL_WIDTHS_MM is a class-average approximation, not a per-instance measurement. There's no way around needing some assumption here without additional sensor data (depth camera, lidar — which you've deliberately ruled out as a hardware dependency) — the question is only how to narrow the error, not eliminate it.
focal_length_px is a solvable, calibratable input — and this is exactly why CameraCalibrationManager reading real CameraCharacteristics per-device (with the hardcoded fallback) matters so much architecturally. Getting this number right is "free" accuracy — it doesn't depend on what's in the scene at all, just on knowing the specific phone's actual camera properties, which Android can tell you, accurately, for most devices.
detected_pixel_width depends on detection quality — a slightly-too-tight or slightly-too-loose bounding box from the object detector directly skews the distance estimate. This is part of why confidence scoring exists alongside the raw distance number (FiducialEstimationResult.confidenceScore) — the system is designed to know when it doesn't know, rather than presenting every estimate with false certainty. This matters enormously for trust, looping back to our credibility discussion — an honest "I'm not fully sure" signal (expressed through guidance phrasing/urgency rather than a literal confidence percentage spoken aloud, since that would be unusable as speech) is safer than confident-sounding wrong information.
